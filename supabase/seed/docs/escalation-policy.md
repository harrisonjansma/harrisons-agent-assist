# Escalation Policy

Use this to decide when and how to move a case beyond front-line support.

## Escalate immediately (priority)
- **Safety:** any report of injury, property damage, overheating, smoke, or a hazardous defect.
- **Security:** suspected account takeover, unauthorized access, or exposed personal data.
- **Legal/regulatory:** mention of a lawyer, a regulator, GDPR/CCPA complaints, or media.
- **Vulnerable customer:** signs of significant distress or risk.

These go to the on-call lead right away, not into the normal queue.

## Escalate through the queue
- Billing disputes and open chargebacks → Billing/Disputes team.
- Refunds over $500 or transactions older than the standard window.
- Enterprise/custom-contract accounts → the account manager.
- Repeated failures on the same order or a suspected product-wide defect/bug.
- Anything you cannot resolve with the tools and policies available.

## How to escalate
1. Summarize the issue in one or two sentences: who, what, and what's already been tried.
2. Attach evidence — order numbers, photos, transaction IDs, prior case links.
3. Set severity honestly. Over-escalating buries the genuinely urgent.
4. Tell the customer what happens next and the realistic timeline. Give them a case number.

## Handling an upset customer
- Acknowledge the frustration first; don't argue the timeline before the customer feels heard.
- Own the next step even if the fix is someone else's — the customer should never have to chase.
- If the customer becomes abusive, stay professional and follow the conduct policy; you may end a call that crosses the line.

## After escalating
- Log the handoff and keep ownership until another team explicitly takes it.
